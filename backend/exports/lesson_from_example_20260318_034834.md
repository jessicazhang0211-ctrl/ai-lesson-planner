### Lesson overview

- **Lesson id**: MATH-Y3-001-A
- **Subject**: Mathematics

- **Key stage**: KS2
- **Topic**: Fractions
- **Subtopic**: Recognising One Half
- **Lesson type**: New Learning
- **Duration**: 45 minutes
- **Curriculum reference**: Recognise, find and write fractions of a discrete set of objects: unit fractions and non-unit fractions with small denominators.

- **Lesson rationale**: This lesson introduces pupils to the idea of one half in a way that matches the thinking style of younger children. The lesson begins with a familiar sharing situation so pupils can connect the maths idea to real life. It then uses clear visual models, simple language, talk-based questioning, hands-on folding, colouring, and a short game. Because pupils at this age often notice that something has been split into two parts without noticing whether the parts are equal, the lesson repeatedly reinforces the idea of equal parts. The overall structure follows a see it, say it, do it, and explain it pattern so that pupils can build confidence step by step.
### Cognitive considerations

#### Age group characteristics

- Pupils learn best from concrete examples before abstract symbols.
- Pupils benefit from repetition and short activity changes.
- Pupils need simple spoken language and clear visual support.
- Pupils often understand fairness before formal fraction notation.
- Pupils may struggle to explain abstract reasoning without sentence support.

#### Design response

- The lesson starts with sharing food fairly.
- The teacher uses pictures, folding, and shading before formal notation.
- Key ideas are repeated several times in different forms.
- Activities alternate between listening, talking, doing, and checking.
- Teacher questions use simple prompts such as 'How do you know?' and 'Are they the same size?'


### Prior learning

#### Knowledge

- Pupils are familiar with sharing objects between two people.
- Pupils can recognise simple 2D shapes such as circles, squares, and rectangles.
- Pupils understand everyday words such as whole, piece, and same.

#### Skills

- Pupils can count small groups of objects.
- Pupils can colour or shade simple shapes.
- Pupils can talk to a partner and answer simple class questions.

- **Connections to previous learning**: This lesson builds on pupils' early understanding of fair sharing and prepares them for later learning about quarters, thirds, and finding fractions of quantities.

### Learning objectives

- Recognise one half as one of two equal parts of a whole.
- Spot when a shape or object has been split into halves correctly.
- Use simple maths words such as half, whole, equal, part, and fraction.
- Show one half by folding, shading, or drawing.

### Success criteria

- I can say that one half means one of two equal parts.
- I can tell if two parts are the same size.
- I can colour one half of a shape correctly.
- I can say why something is or is not one half.

### Key vocabulary

#### Item 1

- **Term**: fraction
- **Definition**: A number that shows part of a whole.
- **Child example**: One half is a fraction.

#### Item 2

- **Term**: half
- **Definition**: One of two equal parts.
- **Child example**: Half a pizza means one of two equal pieces.

#### Item 3

- **Term**: whole
- **Definition**: The complete thing before it is split.
- **Child example**: The full cake is one whole cake.

#### Item 4

- **Term**: equal
- **Definition**: The same size or amount.
- **Child example**: Both pieces are equal because they are the same size.

#### Item 5

- **Term**: part
- **Definition**: A piece of a whole.
- **Child example**: One slice is one part of the pizza.

### Anticipated misconceptions

#### Item 1

- **Issue**: Pupils may think any shape split into two parts shows one half.
- **Response**: The teacher repeatedly checks whether the two parts are the same size.

#### Item 2

- **Issue**: Pupils may think one half only works when a shape is split vertically.
- **Response**: The teacher shows horizontal, diagonal, and other equal splits.

#### Item 3

- **Issue**: Pupils may focus on one shaded part and ignore the total number of parts.
- **Response**: The teacher asks both 'How many parts are there?' and 'Are they equal?'

#### Item 4

- **Issue**: Pupils may use informal language without understanding the fraction meaning.
- **Response**: The teacher models and repeats simple sentence stems using maths vocabulary.

### Interactive activity

- **Type**: game
- **Name**: Find the Half
- **Description**: The teacher shows pictures quickly on the board. Pupils point, hold up a card, or use thumbs up/down to show whether the picture shows one half.
- **Purpose**: To give repeated, fast-paced practice in spotting correct and incorrect examples.

### Teaching sequence

#### Starter

- **Phase**: Starter
- **Duration minutes**: 5
- **Purpose**: To connect the lesson to a familiar real-life idea of fair sharing.
##### Teacher actions

- Show a large picture of one pizza or sandwich on the board.
- Say, 'Two children want to share this fairly. What should happen?'
- Give pupils 20 to 30 seconds to talk to a partner.
- Take a few answers from the class.
- Repeat the key idea that fair means the same size.
- Introduce the word half.

##### Student activities

- Look at the picture.
- Talk to a partner.
- Say that each person should get the same amount.
- Begin to use the word half.

##### Teacher questions

- How can we share this fairly?
- What does fair mean?
- Do both people need the same amount?
- Would it still be fair if one piece was bigger?

##### Expected pupil responses

- Cut it into two pieces.
- Both people should get the same amount.
- Each person gets half.
- No, it is not fair if one piece is bigger.

##### Language focus

- fair
- same size
- equal
- half

##### Assessment opportunities

- Check whether pupils connect half with equal sharing.
- Listen for children who say 'two pieces' but do not mention same size.


#### Teaching Input

- **Phase**: Teaching Input
- **Duration minutes**: 10
- **Purpose**: To teach the meaning of one half using simple visual models and child-friendly explanation.
##### Teacher actions

- Show one whole circle on the board and say, 'This is one whole.'
- Split it into two equal parts and shade one part.
- Write 1/2 and read it aloud as 'one half'.
- Say, 'One half means one of two equal parts.'
- Point to each part and compare sizes.
- Then show a wrong example where one side is bigger and ask if it is half.
- Repeat the phrase 'equal parts' several times.

##### Student activities

- Look at the whole shape.
- Watch it split into two equal parts.
- Say 'one half' aloud.
- Answer whether examples are right or wrong.

- **Teacher explanation**: A whole can be split into parts. If the whole is split into two equal parts, one of those parts is called one half. The parts must be the same size. If one part is bigger, it is not one half.
##### Board content

- 1 whole
- 2 equal parts
- 1/2 = one half
- same size = equal

##### Teacher questions

- How many parts are there?
- Are they the same size?
- Why is this one half?
- Why is this not one half?

##### Expected pupil responses

- There are two parts.
- Yes, they are the same size.
- It is one half because it is one of two equal parts.
- It is not one half because one side is bigger.

##### Assessment opportunities

- Use thumbs up/down after each example.
- Ask individual pupils to finish the sentence stem.


#### Interactive Check

- **Phase**: Interactive Check
- **Duration minutes**: 5
- **Purpose**: To reinforce understanding through a fast, playful game.
##### Teacher actions

- Play the game 'Find the Half'.
- Show several pictures one by one on the board.
- Ask pupils to show thumbs up if it is one half and thumbs down if it is not.
- Pause after each slide and ask one or two pupils to explain.

##### Student activities

- Look carefully at each picture.
- Respond quickly with thumbs up/down or cards.
- Say why an example is right or wrong.

##### Teacher questions

- Is this one half?
- How do you know?
- Are the parts equal?

##### Expected pupil responses

- Yes, because the two parts are equal.
- No, because the shape has three parts.
- No, because one part is bigger.

##### Assessment opportunities

- Quickly identify children who are still unsure.
- Use explanations to check understanding beyond guessing.


#### Guided Practice

- **Phase**: Guided Practice
- **Duration minutes**: 8
- **Purpose**: To support pupils in recognising one half with teacher guidance before independent work.
##### Teacher actions

- Show several shapes on the board.
- Model how to check: count the parts, compare the size, then decide.
- Complete one example together as a class.
- Ask pupils to talk to a partner before answering.

##### Student activities

- Join in with teacher-led checking.
- Say whether a picture shows one half.
- Use sentence stems with a partner.

##### Teacher questions

- First, how many parts are there?
- Next, are they equal?
- So, does it show one half?

##### Expected pupil responses

- There are two parts.
- They are equal.
- Yes, it shows one half.

##### Support strategies

- Point to each section as pupils count.
- Use simple repeated language.
- Give sentence stems on the board.

##### Assessment opportunities

- Listen for correct use of the checking method.
- Notice who still needs concrete support.


#### Hands-on Main Activity

- **Phase**: Hands-on Main Activity
- **Duration minutes**: 12
- **Purpose**: To let pupils physically and visually represent one half.
##### Teacher actions

- Give out paper circles, rectangles, and worksheets.
- Model folding one paper shape into two equal parts.
- Show how to colour one half carefully.
- Explain worksheet tasks one at a time.
- Move around the classroom and provide immediate feedback.

##### Student activities

- Fold shapes into two equal parts.
- Open the shapes and colour one half.
- Complete worksheet questions.
- Circle correct examples and cross incorrect ones.

##### Independent tasks

- Task 1: Fold a paper circle into two equal parts and colour one half.
- Task 2: Shade one half of simple printed shapes.
- Task 3: Circle pictures that show one half correctly.
- Task 4: Cross out pictures that do not show one half.

##### Support adaptations

- Use pre-folded shapes.
- Reduce the number of worksheet items.
- Check each response with the pupil immediately.

##### Challenge extension

- Draw your own shape and show one half in a different way.
- Find half of 10 counters.
- Explain whether a triangle can show one half.

##### Expected outcomes

- Most pupils correctly colour one half of a familiar shape.
- Most pupils identify when parts are not equal.
- Some pupils apply the idea to sets of objects.

##### Assessment opportunities

- Observe folding accuracy.
- Check colouring and worksheet answers.
- Ask pupils to explain one answer verbally.


#### Plenary

- **Phase**: Plenary
- **Duration minutes**: 5
- **Purpose**: To review learning and strengthen memory through simple repetition and explanation.
##### Teacher actions

- Bring the class back together.
- Ask pupils to repeat the key sentence: 'One half means one of two equal parts.'
- Invite pupils to give examples from everyday life.
- Review one correct and one incorrect example quickly.
- Praise accurate vocabulary and clear explanations.

##### Student activities

- Say the key sentence together.
- Give examples such as half a sandwich or half a pizza.
- Answer simple recap questions.

##### Teacher questions

- What does one half mean?
- What word tells us the parts must be the same size?
- Can you tell me one real-life example of half?

##### Expected pupil responses

- One half means one of two equal parts.
- Equal.
- Half a sandwich, half a pizza, half a cake.

##### Assessment opportunities

- Check whole-class confidence.
- Identify pupils who may need more practice next lesson.


### Differentiation

#### Support

- **Description**: Pupils who need extra support will work with concrete materials, such as folded paper shapes, and may complete fewer questions. The teacher or teaching assistant will use short prompts and repeated questions.
##### Strategies

- Pre-folded shapes
- Reduced worksheet load
- Adult guidance
- Visual sentence stems


#### Core

- **Description**: Most pupils will complete the full set of shape-based tasks and explain whether examples show one half using the taught vocabulary.
##### Strategies

- Full worksheet
- Partner discussion
- Verbal explanation


#### Challenge

- **Description**: Higher-attaining pupils will apply the idea of one half to sets of objects and explain why half can look different in different shapes.
##### Strategies

- Object-based fraction tasks
- Open-ended drawing task
- Reasoning questions



### Assessment for learning

- **Informal questioning**: Simple oral questions are used throughout the lesson to check whether pupils understand equal parts.
- **Live visual checks**: Thumbs up/down and mini whiteboards help the teacher see who understands quickly.
- **Observation**: The teacher watches how pupils fold, shade, and discuss examples during guided and independent work.
- **Worksheet evidence**: Completed colouring and identification tasks show whether pupils can apply the idea independently.
- **Spoken reasoning**: Pupils explain in simple language why something is or is not one half.

### Homework

- **Main task**: Draw three everyday objects and show one half of each.
- **Written reflection**: Finish the sentence: One half means...
- **Extension**: Find one example of half at home and tell the class next lesson.

### Teacher reflection prompts

- Did pupils understand that the two parts must be equal, not just two pieces?
- Which visual examples worked best for this age group?
- Did the game improve confidence and participation?
- Were the sentence stems enough to support spoken reasoning?
- Which pupils are ready for learning about quarters next?
